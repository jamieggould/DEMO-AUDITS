Circular Economy Principles

Current and future trends point toward the need for a fundamental shift in the way resources are consumed. A shift to a circular economy will provide considerable economic opportunities as a result.

In contrast to a linear economy (take, make, dispose), a circular economy keeps products and materials circulating through the system at their highest value for as long as possible, through re-use, recycling, refurbishment and remanufacturing. As 60% of total UK waste is generated from construction, demolition and excavation (Defra and Government Statistical Service, 2019) this transition from linear to circular is essential.

The successful implementation of circular economy principles will help to reduce the volume of waste that London produces and has to manage.

A key way of achieving this will be through incorporating circular economy principles into the design of developments, these principals are as follows:

Building in layers

Ensuring that different parts of the building are accessible and can be maintained and replaced where necessary.

Designing out waste

Ensuring that waste reduction is planned in from project inception to completion, including consideration of standardised components, modular build and re-use of secondary products and materials.

Designing for longevity

Creating a built asset with well-defined long-term needs, while being durable, resilient or able to cope with societal and environmental change.

Designing for adaptability or flexibility

To meet the needs of the present but with consideration of how those needs might change in the future.

Selecting materials

Any new material specified in the development should aim to be low impact materials with little or no adverse effect on either the environment or on human health throughout its lifecycle.

Implementing Circular Economy

Building circularity into projects is complex. There are many different approaches a project can identify and adopt solutions, with input from all design team members, including project managers and cost consultants.

A noted previously, the Mayor's updated London Plan will require all new referable developments to submit a Circular Economy Statement. This policy focuses on waste reduction, increases in material re-use and recycling and aim to be net zero-waste.

For circular principles to be successful, it requires a whole building approach.

Design Stage
  • Specify materials that can be disassembled and reused at end of life
  • Avoid composite or laminated materials where alternatives exist
  • Create material passports for all materials
  • Design for adaptability and flexibility of use
Construction Stage
  • Implement a robust Site Waste Management Plan
  • Segregate waste streams for maximum diversion from landfill
  • Monitor and report waste performance against KPIs
End of Life
  • Carry out a Pre-Demolition Audit to identify reuse opportunities
  • Engage specialist contractors for careful deconstruction
  • Identify local reuse markets and social enterprises
  • Record and track all material flows
  • Report on diversion rates achieved

BREEAM Connection: This Pre-Demolition Audit directly supports achievement of BREEAM Waste credits (Wst 01) by quantifying waste arisings and demonstrating a commitment to diverting materials from landfill.

Circular Economy Statement: The findings of this audit should be used to prepare a Circular Economy Statement for submission with any planning application. This report demonstrates commitment to circular economy principles at the pre-demolition stage.

Design Principles

This section will review circular economy design principles and detail measures the proposed development could implement throughout the design, construction and operational phases in addition to those company objectives that are already in place.

Designing Out Waste
Minimised Material Use

Adopting a design approach that focuses on material resource efficiency so that less material is used in the design (i.e. lean design), and / or less waste is produced in the construction process, without compromising the design concept. For waste reduction, minimisation of excavation, simplification and standardisation of materials and components of choice, and dimensional coordination have been considered.

The development will aim to 'design out' waste through the consideration of material specification, such as maximising use of existing materials, and construction techniques in order to prevent and minimise waste generation.

When selecting and designing components the following will be applied where feasible:

  • Design out the need for the component or material.
  • Use reclaimed material over new and remanufactured components over new, where possible.
  • Use products with labels such as Cradle to Cradle (C2C) and Natureplus.
  • Select products that can be remanufactured or reused at end of first life.
  • Use materials with recycled content.
  • Select products that are designed for disassembly.
  • Select materials that can be recycled or composted at end of life.
  • Consider leasing short lived components.

When applying the above, complete transparency and visibility throughout the supply chain will be encouraged. Early engagement with the contractor and partnering within the supply chain will be required.

The Waste Hierarchy

The waste hierarchy sets out the preferred order of waste management options, from the most environmentally favourable (prevention) to the least (disposal). The Lawmens approach to pre-demolition auditing is structured around this hierarchy.

1
PREVENTION — Reduce the amount of waste generated. Retain and reuse the building in its existing form where possible.
2
PREPARING FOR REUSE — Check, clean or repair products so they can be reused. Identify markets for salvaged materials.
3
RECYCLING — Turn waste into new materials. Engage specialist recycling contractors for each material stream.
4
RECOVERY — Includes composting, anaerobic digestion and energy from waste. Preferred to disposal.
5
DISPOSAL — Landfill. The least preferred option. To be minimised as far as practicable.

Lawmens Approach: For each material stream identified in this audit, we have assessed the highest achievable level of the waste hierarchy and provided specific recommendations to support the project team in achieving this outcome.

Legislative Context: The Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC), as transposed into UK law via the Waste (England and Wales) Regulations 2011, places a legal duty on waste producers to apply the waste hierarchy. This audit supports compliance with these obligations.

Early Investigations

Early engagement with the waste management process is critical to maximising material diversion opportunities. A number of preliminary investigations should be undertaken as soon as the decision to demolish or significantly refurbish a building has been made.

Desk-Based Investigations
  • Review of original construction drawings and specifications
  • Review of any existing building surveys (asbestos, condition, etc.)
  • Research into the building's construction history and any known alterations
  • Identification of potential hazardous materials (asbestos, lead paint, PCBs)
  • Review of planning conditions and local authority waste management requirements
  • Research into local reuse markets and social enterprises
Site Investigations
  • Visual inspection of the building fabric, structure and services
  • Identification and quantification of key material streams
  • Assessment of material condition and suitability for reuse
  • Photography of all significant material streams
Stakeholder Engagement
  • Interviews with building manager and facilities management team
  • Engagement with demolition contractors to understand sequencing
  • Consultation with local reuse organisations and salvage dealers
  • Engagement with the design team regarding new build specification

Hazardous Materials: The presence of asbestos or other hazardous materials can significantly affect the viability of material reuse. All identified hazardous materials have been noted within this report and should be appropriately managed prior to any demolition or stripping works.

Methodology

This Pre-Demolition Audit has been prepared by Lawmens using a combination of desk-based research, site investigation, and stakeholder consultation. The methodology has been developed in accordance with BS 5502, WRAP's guidance on pre-demolition auditing, and BREEAM UK assessment requirements.

Data Collection

Material quantities have been estimated from a combination of available drawings, specifications, and visual assessment during site visits. Where quantities could not be determined from drawings, standard conversion factors from the RICS and BRE Digest have been applied. All estimates should be treated as indicative.

Carbon Factors

Embodied carbon factors used in this report are taken from the Inventory of Carbon and Energy (ICE) database (University of Bath) and the WRAP embodied carbon database. Figures represent Scope A1–A3 embodied carbon (cradle to gate).

Limitations

This audit is based on visual inspection and available information at the time of survey. Concealed materials have been estimated based on drawings and general building type. The presence of asbestos or other hazardous materials may affect the viability of material reuse — a full asbestos survey should be completed prior to any deconstruction works. All quantities are indicative and may vary from actual arisings.

Assumptions
  • Weights have been calculated using standard bulk density figures from CIRIA and WRAP.
  • Volume estimates are based on dimension take-offs from available drawings.
  • Diversion rates are indicative targets based on current market conditions.
  • Carbon factors represent industry averages and actual factors may vary depending on material specification and origin.
