Pre-Demolition Audits are surveys undertaken prior to demolition or major refurbishment in order to identify, quantify and characterise materials present within a building or structure. The primary aim is to identify opportunities for materials to be diverted from landfill through reuse, recycling or recovery, thereby reducing the environmental impact of the project and contributing to broader sustainability objectives.

The Circular Economy

The circular economy (CE) represents a systemic approach to economic development designed to benefit businesses, society, and the environment. In contrast to the traditional 'take-make-waste' linear model, a circular economy aims to be restorative and regenerative by design — keeping products, components and materials at their highest value at all times, whilst distinguishing between technical and biological cycles.

The construction and demolition sector is one of the largest generators of waste in the UK, accounting for approximately 60% of total waste arisings. The adoption of circular economy principles offers significant potential to reduce this impact, conserve natural resources, reduce carbon emissions, and create new economic opportunities through the development of secondary material markets.

Linear Economy

1TAKE — Extract raw materials
2MAKE — Manufacture products
3WASTE — Dispose of at end of life

Circular Economy

↑REGENERATE — Restore natural systems
↑SHARE — Maximise product use
↑OPTIMISE — Extend product life
↑LOOP — Reuse & recycle materials
↑VIRTUALISE — Dematerialise

The UK Government's Resources and Waste Strategy (2018) sets out ambitious targets for moving towards a more circular economy, including a commitment to eliminating avoidable waste by 2050. Pre-demolition audits are a key tool in supporting this transition within the construction sector.

Implementing Circular Economy in Construction

Implementing circular economy principles within construction projects requires a whole-life approach to materials management, beginning at the design stage and continuing through construction, operation and end-of-life. Key strategies include design for disassembly, material passports, and procurement of materials with high recycled content.

Design Stage

  • Specify materials that can be disassembled and reused at end of life
  • Avoid composite or laminated materials where alternatives exist
  • Create material passports recording quantity, quality and location of all materials
  • Design for adaptability and flexibility of use
  • Specify materials with high recycled content

Construction Stage

  • Implement a robust Site Waste Management Plan
  • Segregate waste streams for maximum diversion from landfill
  • Reuse excavated material on site where possible
  • Monitor and report waste performance against KPIs

End of Life

  • Carry out a Pre-Demolition Audit to identify reuse opportunities
  • Engage specialist contractors for careful deconstruction
  • Identify local reuse markets and social enterprises
  • Record and track all material flows
  • Report on diversion rates achieved

BREEAM Connection: This Pre-Demolition Audit directly supports achievement of BREEAM Waste credits (Wst 01) by quantifying waste arisings and demonstrating a commitment to diverting materials from landfill.

Design Principles for Deconstruction

Design for Deconstruction (DfD) is a set of design strategies that facilitate the efficient disassembly of buildings at end of life, enabling the recovery of materials and components for reuse or recycling. The following principles should be applied to new construction works on this site.

Connection Strategies

  • Use mechanical fixings (bolts, screws) in preference to adhesives or welding
  • Avoid composite connections between dissimilar materials
  • Ensure connections are accessible and visible for disassembly
  • Limit the number of different connection types used
  • Provide clear documentation of all connection details

Structural Strategies

  • Use modular, standardised structural components
  • Avoid in-situ concrete where precast alternatives exist
  • Design for adaptability to future uses
  • Separate structure from services and fit-out

Material Strategies

  • Specify materials with known provenance and composition
  • Avoid hazardous materials (asbestos, PFAS, lead paint)
  • Use materials with established recycling routes
  • Specify reclaimed or recycled materials where possible
  • Maintain material passports throughout the building lifecycle

Documentation

  • Create and maintain a building material passport
  • Record quantities, specifications and locations of all materials
  • Document disassembly sequences
  • Update records following any alterations or repairs

The Waste Hierarchy

The waste hierarchy sets out the preferred order of waste management options, from the most environmentally favourable (prevention) to the least (disposal). The Lawmens approach to pre-demolition auditing is structured around this hierarchy, with the aim of applying the highest possible option to each material stream identified.

1
PREVENTION — Reduce the amount of waste generated. Retain and reuse the building in its existing form where possible.
2
PREPARING FOR REUSE — Check, clean or repair products so they can be reused. Identify markets for salvaged materials.
3
RECYCLING — Turn waste into new materials. Engage specialist recycling contractors for each material stream.
4
RECOVERY — Includes composting, anaerobic digestion and energy from waste. Preferred to disposal.
5
DISPOSAL — Landfill. The least preferred option. To be minimised as far as practicable.

Lawmens Approach: For each material stream identified in this audit, we have assessed the highest achievable level of the waste hierarchy and provided specific recommendations to support the project team in achieving this outcome.

Legislative Context: The Waste Framework Directive (2008/98/EC), as transposed into UK law via the Waste (England and Wales) Regulations 2011, places a legal duty on waste producers to apply the waste hierarchy. This audit supports compliance with these obligations.

Early Investigations

Early engagement with the waste management process is critical to maximising material diversion opportunities. A number of preliminary investigations should be undertaken as soon as the decision to demolish or significantly refurbish a building has been made.

Desk-Based Investigations

  • Review of original construction drawings and specifications
  • Review of any existing building surveys (asbestos, condition, etc.)
  • Research into the building's construction history and any known alterations
  • Identification of potential hazardous materials (asbestos, lead paint, PCBs)
  • Review of planning conditions and local authority waste management requirements
  • Research into local reuse markets and social enterprises

Site Investigations

  • Visual inspection of the building fabric, structure and services
  • Identification and quantification of key material streams
  • Assessment of material condition and suitability for reuse
  • Photography of all significant material streams

Stakeholder Engagement

  • Interviews with building manager and facilities management team
  • Engagement with demolition contractors to understand sequencing
  • Consultation with local reuse organisations and salvage dealers
  • Engagement with the design team regarding new build specification

Hazardous Materials: The presence of asbestos or other hazardous materials can significantly affect the viability of material reuse. All identified hazardous materials have been noted within this report and should be appropriately managed prior to any demolition or stripping works.

Methodology

This Pre-Demolition Audit has been prepared by Lawmens using a combination of desk-based research, site investigation, and stakeholder consultation. The methodology has been developed in accordance with BS 5502, WRAP's guidance on pre-demolition auditing, and BREEAM UK assessment requirements.

Data Collection

Material quantities have been estimated from a combination of available drawings, specifications, and visual assessment during site visits. Where quantities could not be determined from drawings, standard conversion factors from the RICS and BRE Digest have been applied. All estimates should be treated as indicative — final quantities will depend on the actual demolition programme and findings during works.

Carbon Factors

Embodied carbon factors used in this report are taken from the Inventory of Carbon and Energy (ICE) database (University of Bath) and the WRAP embodied carbon database. Figures represent Scope A1–A3 embodied carbon (cradle to gate). The embodied carbon savings from reuse and recycling are presented as indicative figures to support decision-making.

Limitations

This audit is based on visual inspection and available information at the time of survey. Concealed materials (e.g. behind finishes or within floor/ceiling voids) have been estimated based on drawings and general building type. The presence of asbestos or other hazardous materials may affect the viability of material reuse — a full asbestos survey should be completed prior to any deconstruction works. All quantities are indicative and may vary from actual arisings.

Assumptions

  • Weights have been calculated using standard bulk density figures from CIRIA and WRAP.
  • Volume estimates are based on dimension take-offs from available drawings.
  • Diversion rates are indicative targets based on current market conditions and represent achievable levels with appropriate planning and contractor engagement.
  • Carbon factors represent industry averages and actual factors may vary depending on material specification and origin.
